# Data Flywheel Map

> Score each loop 1–5. Your weakest loop is where competitors attack first.
> The four loops below are the M2 starting point — adapt if your product has 2 or 6 loops instead of 4.

## Flywheel Loops

| Loop | What It Measures | Score 1 | Score 5 | Score |
|------|------------------|---------|---------|-------|
| **Correction** | Do users fix AI outputs? Is that signal captured and reused? | No capture | Automated retraining | 3/5 |
| **Preference** | Does the product learn individual / team preferences over time? | Stateless | Deep personalization | 3/5 |
| **Domain Context** | Does usage in one area improve quality in adjacent areas? | Siloed | Cross-domain transfer | 3/5 |
| **Network** | Does each new user / team make the product better for everyone? | Isolated | Strong network effects | 2/5 |

### Correction Loop — 3/5
**What you capture today:** Every compliance flag Governor surfaces is either acted on or ignored by the operator. When a deployment manager dismisses a flag as a false positive, or escalates one as a real risk, that judgement is a correction signal. The eight compliance categories (LEGAL, MEDICAL, FIN, PII, HALLUC, TONE, BIAS, SAFETY) give the signal structure — it's not a vague thumbs-up/down but a typed, categorised verdict. Governor also surfaces a suggested rewrite for every flagged robot reply — when a manager accepts or modifies that suggestion, it is a high-quality correction signal: the exact problematic span, the category, and the preferred alternative are all captured in a single interaction.

**How it compounds:** Each accepted/dismissed flag and each acted-on suggested rewrite trains the compliance model to reduce false positives and catch genuine risks more reliably. Over time, Governor gets measurably more accurate for each customer's specific deployment scenario — a hotel front desk model that has seen 10,000 flagged moments and 2,000 accepted rewrites behaves very differently from a fresh install.

### Preference Loop — 3/5
**What you capture today:** Governor captures a richer preference signal than previously assessed. The scenario system — where operators write a plain-English brief describing the robot's job, venue, and rules, and Governor uses the AI to turn it into a system prompt — is a direct preference capture mechanism. Each time an operator edits, refines, or switches a scenario, they are expressing a preference about what their robot should and should not do. These scenario edits accumulate as a per-customer configuration history. Presets for common deployments (hotel, hospital, museum) provide a starting point; custom scenarios encode organisation-specific rules that no generic tool would know.

**How it compounds:** As scenario history grows, Governor can begin to anticipate compliance risk before it happens — a new hotel customer's scenario brief shares structural characteristics with hundreds of past hotel scenarios, so Governor can pre-populate likely risk categories and flag patterns the customer hasn't encountered yet. The scenario library also becomes a proprietary asset: a curated, battle-tested set of deployment configurations that improves with every new vertical added. Switching to a competitor means losing that configuration history and starting from generic defaults.

---

### Domain Context Loop — 3/5
**What you capture today:** The eight compliance categories are horizontally consistent across all deployment contexts. A MEDICAL flag generated by a hotel guest asking about a twisted ankle uses the same model as a MEDICAL flag in a school nurse scenario. Every customer's data implicitly trains the same underlying compliance classifiers, even if the deployment contexts differ.

**How it compounds:** Compliance signals from hospitality deployments improve the models for all hospitality customers. Edge cases discovered in museum deployments surface risks that corporate customers haven't encountered yet. The category structure makes cross-domain transfer tractable — because the risk types are universal even when the contexts differ. The moat deepens: a customer in a new vertical benefits immediately from everything Governor has learned in adjacent verticals.

---

### Network Loop — 2/5
**What you capture today:** No network effect is currently active — each deployment is isolated. However, the structural conditions for a strong network effect exist: every red-teaming session any customer runs is a potential contribution to a shared adversarial input library. The more operators attempt to stress-test their robots (as shown in the prototype with STRESS-TEST labelled inputs), the richer the shared dataset of edge cases becomes.

**How it compounds:** Once anonymised cross-customer learning is enabled, each new Engineered Arts customer using Governor makes the compliance models better for every other customer. A museum in Tokyo that discovers a novel adversarial prompt pattern immediately benefits a hotel in London. This is the flywheel loop that ultimately makes Governor impossible to replicate — a competitor starting from zero has no historical HRI safety data to train from. **Activating this loop is the highest-leverage investment on the roadmap.**

---

**Total Flywheel Score: 11/20**
**Weakest Loop:** Network (2/5) — no cross-customer learning is currently active, meaning the data advantage is accumulating but not yet compounding across the customer base.
**Fix for weakest loop:** Build anonymised session contribution — operators opt in to share flagged moments (stripped of PII) into a shared compliance corpus. Even a small initial cohort of opted-in customers begins generating the network effect. Pair with a "Benchmark" feature showing operators how their robot's risk profile compares to anonymised industry peers — this creates pull for contribution.

## Encroachment Threat Assessment

### 1. Platform Encroachment
**Attacker:** OpenAI / Anthropic
**Vector:** Ship native guardrail and compliance APIs (building on existing moderation tooling) that work across any LLM-powered product — priced at cost or bundled with API usage to establish the standard. Target enterprise legal and compliance teams directly.
**Time-to-threat:** 12–18 months
**% of value at risk:** ~35% — the generic compliance classification layer is replicable; the embodied HRI data, robot-specific parity, and live monitoring integration are not.

### 2. Vertical Competitor
**Attacker:** ElevenLabs
**Vector:** Expand their voice agent platform to include a compliance monitoring layer for conversational AI deployments. Customers who treat the robot as "just a voice agent with a face" adopt ElevenLabs as a cheaper substitute for pre-deployment simulation testing.
**Time-to-threat:** 6–12 months
**% of value at risk:** ~25% — simulation layer only; ElevenLabs cannot monitor live deployed Engineered Arts robots or achieve embodied behavioural parity.

### 3. Adjacent Expansion
**Attacker:** LLM observability platforms (e.g. LangSmith, Braintrust, Helicone)
**Vector:** Expand from developer-focused LLM tracing into enterprise compliance monitoring — they already have the conversation logging infrastructure and are moving up-market. Could add robot-specific adapters.
**Time-to-threat:** 18–24 months
**% of value at risk:** ~20% — post-hoc analysis layer only; no simulation capability or embodied parity.

## 90-Day Encroachment Plan

*Your partner played the Big Tech attacker. What was their plan to kill you?*

**Attacker:** OpenAI

**Attack vector (target the weakest loop):** The Network loop is unactivated — Governor's data advantage is accumulating in silos. OpenAI attacks before the network effect compounds, when the data moat is still shallow.

**Weeks 1–4 — what they ship:** Launch a "Compliance API" — pre-built guardrails for LEGAL, MEDICAL, FINANCIAL, PII, and SAFETY categories, available to any LLM-powered product. Announce SOC2 compliance and GDPR/COPPA pre-configuration. Price it as a free tier on top of existing API usage.

**Weeks 5–8 — how they poach users:** Reach out directly to enterprise procurement teams at Engineered Arts customers. Offer to integrate the Compliance API as a wrapper around the robot's existing LLM — no simulation required, no new platform to learn. "You're already using our models. Compliance is now included."

**Weeks 9–12 — why users don't come back:** Enterprise compliance teams standardise on OpenAI's audit logs and regulatory templates. Governor's compliance categories feel redundant when the underlying LLM already handles moderation. Without the Network loop activated, Governor can't demonstrate that its robot-specific data catches risks that generic APIs miss.

**Your defense:** Activate the Network loop immediately — launch anonymised cross-customer benchmarking before the 90-day window opens. Publish a "Governor vs. Generic Guardrails" comparison showing real cases where embodied, physical-world HRI context produced compliance risks that OpenAI's moderation API scored as clean. Make the embodied difference empirically visible, not just a narrative claim.
